The headline: Daryl Morey is out.

After six seasons as the Sixers' president of basketball operations, Morey will no longer have that post after meeting with owners Josh Harris and David Blitzer on Tuesday. ESPN's Shams Charania was the first to report the news. Head coach Nick Nurse will be back for his fourth season and Bob Myers, the former Golden State Warriors' executive who is the president of sports for HBSE, will lead the team's search for a new president of basketball operations.

Six years of the Morey era — three of them defined by the second-round exit ceiling, one of them by the first-round comeback against Boston this year, all of them by Embiid's availability.

The Philadelphia 76ers and Daryl Morey agreed to "part ways" Tuesday, marking the franchise's first major offseason move. The organization will simultaneously search for Morey's replacement while preparing for the NBA Draft and potentially drastic roster changes, as the Sixers currently have just six players on guaranteed contracts for next season.

Six players on guaranteed contracts. Sit with that. The Sixers' roster is in a state of significant flux, and a brand-new front-office voice will be making the calls on Kelly Oubre's future, Quentin Grimes's future, Dominick Barlow's option, and the deeper trade-market questions about Paul George and even Embiid.

Bob Myers running the search is an interesting move.

Philadelphia announced that former Golden State Warriors executive Bob Myers will take over on what is expected to be an interim basis and will lead the search for Morey's replacement. Myers held various executive positions during the Warriors' dynasty run, which included five consecutive Finals runs, six total Finals appearances and four championships. Myers could likely have the position if he wanted, but his role frankly extends beyond basketball these days.

Myers is the architect of the Warriors dynasty. The credibility he brings to this process should attract every serious executive in the league to take the call.

There is a press conference today at four p.m., with both Josh Harris and Myers speaking. That is going to be must-listen, because we should learn the philosophy of the search. Are they looking for a former GM with a championship pedigree? A young analytical mind? Someone with strong cap and CBA expertise? The Sixers' job is one of the most complicated in the league because of the constraints — Embiid's contract, George's contract, and the diminishing draft-pick capital. Whoever takes it has to be comfortable with hard problems.

ESPN had a thoughtful piece this week that framed the structural challenge.

It's the latest reminder of how fragile the 76ers will always be with Embiid as their foundation. As Philadelphia enters the offseason via its sixth second-round exit in nine playoffs during the Embiid era -- and without a president of basketball operations -- it's unclear how a franchise with few options to improve moves forward.

Six second-round exits in nine years. That is the Embiid résumé, and it is not damning, but it is bounded. The new president has to decide: are they trying to break through that ceiling with this core, or are they reframing the timeline around Maxey and VJ Edgecombe and accepting that the championship window may sit a couple years out?

In Game 2 against Boston in the first round, Edgecombe put on another show. He became the first rookie since Tim Duncan -- and the youngest ever, surpassing Magic Johnson -- to record at least 30 points and 10 rebounds in a playoff game.

Younger than Magic Johnson at the time. That is the kind of foundation piece that changes how you think about the franchise. Edgecombe finished third in Rookie of the Year voting, paired with Maxey in the backcourt, and you can squint and see a future where this team is built around those two and not built down from Embiid.

Now, Paul George. This is the contract that complicates everything.

The 36-year-old George is set to make $54.1 million next season with a $56.5 million player option in 2027-28, which we can all but guarantee he picks up.

Fifty-four million next year, then fifty-six the year after. That is a lot of money for a thirty-six-year-old who has already had availability issues. The new president of basketball operations is going to have to make a defining call on George, and there is no clean answer.

The free-agent decisions matter, too. Kelly Oubre, Quentin Grimes, Andre Drummond all hit the open market. The Sixers, per ESPN's Bobby Marks, sit roughly fourteen and a half million below the tax before re-signing those guys. Bring them all back, and you are in the tax. Let them all walk, and your depth chart has holes you cannot fill at the minimum.

This is the kind of offseason where the front-office hire shapes the next five years. Get the right person, and you can navigate around the difficult contracts and build a credible contender. Get the wrong person — or someone too cautious about the punitive tax, too willing to defer the hard calls — and you waste another year of the Embiid-Maxey-Edgecombe alignment.

The Knicks sweep stung, but this is the bigger story.
