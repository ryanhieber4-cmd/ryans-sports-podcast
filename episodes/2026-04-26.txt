Two hundred and fifty-three days. That is how long Zack Wheeler waited to throw a major-league pitch again, and when he finally did Saturday night in Atlanta, the radar gun in the corner of the screen flashed ninety-five.

Rafael Marchán caught Wheeler's first major-league pitch in 253 days, peeked at the velocity reading on the scoreboard at 95 miles an hour, and had one thought.

The thought, presumably, was that the Phillies finally had their ace back. They needed every bit of him.

After an 8-5 victory over the Braves through a steady rain, they almost needed to be reminded of how to celebrate.

The streak is dead. Ten games of misery, the worst Philadelphia stretch in twenty-seven years, snapped in extra innings against the team with the best record in baseball.

Let's get to the Phillies. The final was eight to five in ten innings, and the surface-level story is that the Phillies snapped their longest losing streak in twenty-seven years, but the real story is what happened in the first inning, the fourth inning, and the tenth inning, in roughly that order of importance.

Marchán caught that first pitch at 95 miles an hour, and given everything Wheeler went through since last August — the blood clot near his shoulder, the diagnosis of thoracic outlet syndrome, surgery to remove a rib and relieve a compressed vein — just seeing their ace on the mound here Saturday night would've qualified as a win.

Wheeler had not pitched since August fifteenth.

His fastball did not reach 95 mph in any of his five rehab starts after he averaged over 96 mph last season. Wheeler's velocity was not an issue Saturday. In his 20-pitch first inning, Wheeler exceeded 95 mph 11 times.

Eleven times. So whatever doubts were creeping in during those rehab outings about whether the velocity would come back, those doubts got buried in the first ten minutes of the game.

The line is going to read: five innings, three hits, two runs, three walks, six strikeouts.

He struck out Ronald Acuña Jr. twice — once with a 96.5 mile-an-hour fastball and the second time with an 82.8 mile-an-hour sweeper. He had five whiffs in three hitless at-bats against the Braves star.

That is a fascinating little line item, because the sweeper is now Wheeler's swing-and-miss weapon and it looked, by the numbers, sharper than ever.

The pitch is becoming another Phillies calling card under pitching coach Caleb Cotham. Of Wheeler's non-fastballs Saturday, the sweeper was the one he threw most, at 14 percent. It had noticeably more bite, and the numbers backed that up. The pitch spun at 2,791 rpm, a jump of 122 rpm from last season. Against right-handed hitters, it also showed a 7-inch increase in drop compared to his 2025 average, according to Statcast.

Seven more inches of drop. That is a different pitch. And the broader chase profile was even more encouraging.

All six of his pitches finished with at least a 20 percent chase rate. His overall chase rate sat at 44 percent.

Forty-four percent chase. For context, his career best over a full season was thirty-three.

Wheeler did get squeezed in the fourth, when the Braves finally caught up to him for two runs.

Wheeler cruised through his first three innings, allowing just one hit and one walk in 35 pitches. But in a 36-pitch fourth inning, he gave up two runs on two hits and two walks. He stranded runners on second and third by striking out Mauricio Dubón and Mike Yastrzemski to end the inning.

He admitted to Rob Thomson that he was tired, that he was treading water, and then he went out for the fifth and gave Thomson a clean inning before turning it over. That sequence — getting hit, surviving, gathering himself, going back out — is the part that managers and pitching coaches care about more than a velocity spike. The arm worked when he asked it to.

Now, the offense. The Phillies entered the night at, well, rock bottom.

They came in with the second-lowest batting average on balls in play in baseball at .257. If that holds, it would be their lowest BABIP in 86 years, since the 1940 Phillies posted a .254 mark.

Eighty-six years. That is the kind of stat line that tells you a team is unlucky, that the contact has been there but the results have not. Saturday, finally, some of the contact found grass.

They needed three triples, including one from Kyle Schwarber after the center fielder fell down. They needed four RBIs from Bryce Harper, including a tiebreaking two-run single through the left side with the bases loaded in a four-run 10th inning. And then, after an 8-5 victory over the Braves through a steady rain, they almost needed to be reminded of how to celebrate.

The tenth inning is the part you tell your kids about.

Trea Turner worked a walk. Schwarber worked another. Harper punched a two-run single through the left side to give the Phillies a 6-4 lead. Brandon Marsh followed with a two-run hit of his own to make it 8-4.

No home runs. Walks, singles, the kind of inning that has felt mythical for two weeks. Adolis García's RBI triple in the first set the tone. Bryson Stott added another in the fourth. Schwarber's tumble-and-scramble triple in the eighth set up a Harper sacrifice fly to tie it. The Phillies finally looked, for nine outs and one extra inning, like the lineup that is supposed to be in this league.

The team is now nine and eighteen. That is still bad. There is no spinning the standings. But the underlying signal Saturday is that this is not a roster that has lost its identity. They have been outscoring their luck for three weeks, and now they finally get their ace back in a rotation that already has Cristopher Sánchez and Aaron Nola at the top. Speaking of Nola: he gets the ball today against Chris Sale at one-thirty-five, the rubber match in Atlanta and the last meeting between these clubs until September. Sale has been Sale. Nola has been searching for the version of himself we saw last summer. If the offense carries the energy from last night into a Sunday matinee, we could be talking tonight about a series win against the best team in the National League.

Let's pivot to the Eagles, because the draft just ended and the way Howie Roseman attacked this weekend told you a story about the roster. The Eagles came into Thursday night with the twenty-third pick. They left it with the twentieth pick and a wide receiver.

The Eagles kicked off the 2026 NFL Draft by moving up three spots in a trade with the Dallas Cowboys to select WR Makai Lemon with the 20th overall pick.

The trade compensation, per the Wikipedia summary aggregating beat reporting:

a first-round selection and two fourth-round selections, the 23rd, 114th and 137th overall, sent to Dallas in exchange for the 20th overall pick.

So they paid a real price to move up three spots, and the league reaction was, broadly, that they paid that price because Lemon is the floor of the post-A.J. Brown plan.

The reactions to the Eagles drafting Makai Lemon after trading up to No. 20 came quick, with many saying that Howie Roseman was able to give the team some insurance should they end up trading A.J. Brown later in the offseason. Analysts around the league are generally positive about the Eagles picking Lemon, with the team earning mostly A grades for the pick, with the lowest opinion landing a C.

Lemon won the Biletnikoff this past year. He is a different player than Brown — smaller, quicker, more of an underneath and intermediate operator. The reporting, especially from the Inquirer and from Bleeding Green, is that this pick is connected to the A.J. Brown situation, which Ryan, you and I have talked about for a month.

"Lemon should be a quality starter for the Eagles and this probably shows that A.J. Brown will be headed elsewhere at the start of the summer."

That is the league consensus, not just one analyst.

But the move I want to spend more time on is the one that happened Friday night, because it is the one that should make you the happiest as a fan. The Eagles traded for Jonathan Greenard. The terms, per the team announcement:

the Eagles acquired outside linebacker Jonathan Greenard and a seventh-round pick in 2026 from the Minnesota Vikings in exchange for a third-round draft pick this year and a 2027 third-round pick.

Two third-round picks for an established edge rusher.

The Eagles are signing Greenard to a four-year extension, keeping him under contract through 2031, according to ESPN. The new contract is reportedly worth $100 million and includes $50 million guaranteed.

Here is why that matters. Seth Walder of ESPN graded the trade an A-minus.

Specifically pointing to the fact that they got Greenard for less than the player he is effectively replacing, Jaelan Phillips, "It's fair to say that the Eagles are getting a legitimate top edge rusher and a major upgrade over Jalyx Hunt to pair with Nolan Smith Jr. The trade compensation is real — two third-round picks is nothing to sneeze at — but they are getting Greenard at what I consider a well-below-market price, signing him to a new four-year, $100 million deal."

So the Eagles upgraded the pass rush, did so at a financial discount because Greenard was willing to extend, and they did it on draft night when most teams were busy doing other things. That is Roseman at his best. The Eagles' pass rush, paired with Nolan Smith on the other side, just got teeth.

The rest of the class. In the second round, they took Eli Stowers, the tight end out of Vanderbilt.

"Will have more thoughts about Eli Stowers, the new Eagles tight end, but he's more of a receiver than true in-line tight end. Mismatch weapon, vertical threat ability, an H-back type that is very similar skill set wise to Evan Engram. Former QB background." Stowers ran a 4.51 40-yard dash at the combine, had a 45-and-a-half-inch vertical leap and an 11-foot, 3-inch broad jump. Those are elite numbers for tight ends, and while Stowers still has some polishing to do, his profile is intriguing.

The Evan Engram comp is interesting because that is exactly the role this offense has been trying to fill since Dallas Goedert started missing time in clusters.

In the third round, the Eagles took Markel Bell out of Miami.

Howie Roseman called 6-foot-9 tackle Markel Bell a "passion player" for the Eagles throughout the draft process. The Eagles selected Bell with the 68th pick. He has rare size at the position and didn't allow a sack in 2025. Roseman said he wondered what might happen if the 21-year-old Bell went back to college for another year. As in, he probably would be a more sought-after prospect next draft cycle.

A developmental tackle. Big swing on a kid with rare measurables. That is a Stoutland pick, except Stoutland is no longer here, and that wrinkle is its own subplot.

This will be the Eagles' first season since 2012 without offensive line coach and run game coordinator Jeff Stoutland, who announced his departure from the organization on February 4. Ryan Mahaffey was named the team's new run game coordinator and tight ends coach.

Chris Kuper is the new offensive line coach. That transition is one of the quieter storylines of this offseason and one that will get loud in a hurry if there is any hiccup up front in September.

The undrafted free agent class came together this morning, and the most prominent name is Deontae Lawson out of Alabama.

Lawson has been a full-time starter for Bama the last three years, and he wore the green dot helmet for their defense. A couple years ago it would have been shocking to learn that Lawson would go undrafted, but he tore an ACL late in 2024 and wasn't quite the same player in 2025, though he did make it back in time for the start of the season.

If his medicals check out and the burst is back by training camp, Lawson is the kind of player who can stick on a depth chart that needed exactly this type of body at off-ball linebacker.

The big picture on this draft: the Eagles got materially better at edge rusher, added two pass-game weapons in Lemon and Stowers, took a project at left tackle, and reinforced a few back-end-of-roster spots. They paid for it with a chunk of mid-round capital, but for a team that is trying to maximize the back end of the Saquon-Hurts-DeVonta window, that is the right trade.

They will attempt to improve on their 11–6 record from the year before, which ended in a Wild Card round defeat by the San Francisco 49ers, 23–19.

That is the bar. Get back to where they were, push past it, and get back to the conference championship. The roster, this morning, is closer to that than it was Thursday afternoon.

Sixers. Game Three was Friday at home, and the final was a hundred and eight to a hundred. Boston now leads two-one.

Tatum continued to shine in his return from injury with 25 points and Jaylen Brown also scored 25 to help the Boston Celtics beat the Philadelphia 76ers 108-100 on Friday night and take a 2-1 lead in the Eastern Conference first-round series. Game 4 is Sunday in Philadelphia. Tatum was 5 of 9 on 3-pointers in just his 19th game this season following surgery last May to repair his Achilles tendon. Brown scored eight straight points late in the fourth for a 96-92 lead that put some distance between them and a Sixers team brimming with confidence following a surprise Game 2 win in Boston.

Tatum at five-of-nine from three is the part that stings. He has been on a minutes restriction, he has been visibly working back into game shape, and Friday night he looked like himself in the fourth quarter.

JT scored 11 of his 25 points while shooting 4-for-5 and 2-for-2 from 3-point range in all 12 minutes of the fourth quarter.

That is the version of Tatum the Celtics were paying for and the version the Sixers were probably hoping had not arrived yet.

For Philadelphia, the headline performance was VJ Edgecombe with the inefficient double-double.

Edgecombe supplied 10 points, 10 rebounds, five assists, one block and one steal across 41 minutes during Friday's 108-100 loss to the Celtics in Game 3 of the first round of the Eastern Conference playoffs. Edgecombe recorded his second straight double-double and his third in the past four games. However, it came on inefficient shooting, as he needed 17 shot attempts to reach just 10 points.

Five-for-seventeen, oh-for-seven from three. The rookie has been plugged into a load he was not built for in April, and he has carried it about as well as any first-year guard could. Tyrese Maxey had twenty-one on eight-of-twenty-one shooting. Kelly Oubre had seventeen. Paul George logged forty-one minutes.

The math of a 2-1 series tells you tonight matters more than any other game in this Sixers run.

Teams that earn a 2-1 lead go on to win an NBA Playoffs series 80% of the time. With a 3-1 lead, it's 95.6% of the time, with 13 teams recovering from such a gap in NBA history. With a 3-0 lead, it's been 100% — no team has ever come back to overcome that deficit.

So Game Four is the swing point. Win, and it is a one-one home-court reset back in Boston. Lose, and the historical odds say this series is essentially over. Tip is at seven Eastern in Philadelphia tonight.

The frustrating part of the Sixers' situation is the Joel Embiid context.

The Celtics took a comfortable 123–91 victory over the short-handed 76ers, who were missing Joel Embiid following appendicitis surgery with an indefinite timeline for return.

Indefinite. That word has been the Sixers' bane for three years now, and it is again.

The Sixers got only a combined 22 points from Embiid replacements Adem Bona and Andre Drummond.

Bona has started every game this series. Drummond has been the older legs. Neither of those rotations is winning a series against Boston, and Nick Nurse knows it. The job tonight, frankly, is to throw something different at the Celtics — maybe more Justin Edwards minutes, maybe more pace — and hope Edgecombe and Maxey can recreate the Game Two magic at home in front of their crowd. If Joel was healthy, this series looks completely different. He is not, and so it is what it is.

To the Flyers, where the broom has been put back in the closet. The Flyers had Pittsburgh down three games to none and a chance to sweep at home. They could not finish the job. The final last night was four to two Pittsburgh, and the Penguins are now headed back to PPG Paints Arena for Game Five on Monday.

Sidney Crosby and Kris Letang each scored goals and the three-time Stanley Cup champion teammates are headed back to Pittsburgh after they helped the Pittsburgh Penguins avoid a first-round series sweep with a 4-2 win over the Philadelphia Flyers in Game 4 on Saturday night. Game 5 is Monday in Pittsburgh.

The two oldest reasons the Flyers' fanbase has hated Pittsburgh for two decades scored the goals that kept their season alive. Of course they did.

The decisive coaching move came before puck drop.

Penguins coach Dan Muse made the right call with the season on the line to bench starting goalie Stuart Skinner in favor of Arturs Silovs, who responded with 25 saves.

Skinner had been the leak.

The Penguins received a solid effort from Silovs in the net after Skinner was ineffective with three losses and an .873 save percentage. Silovs, who went 19-12-8 this season, made his 11th career playoff start; the previous 10 came with Vancouver in 2023-24 when it was coached by Rick Tocchet.

So now Tocchet, on the Flyers' bench this year and the architect of this whole Philadelphia turnaround, is going to face the goalie he last had in Vancouver. There is poetry in that, but only if the Flyers find a way past him on Monday.

The Flyers' goal was not a clean one.

The 38-year-old Crosby, a career-long foil for the Flyers, not only scored his first goal of the series, but he also set a savvy screen in the third period on defenseman Travis Sanheim that allowed Letang to have a clean look when he ripped his first goal of the series past Dan Vladar for a 3-1 lead. The goal was crucial after Travis Konecny scored to make it 3-2 and ignite the "Let's go Flyers!" chants that had largely been dormant with the Flyers down early.

Letang's goal was the dagger. Konecny's gave the Xfinity Mobile Arena crowd one breath. Then Letang took it back.

The piece of this game that will follow Vladar around for at least a couple of nights is the second-period mistake.

Vladar, voted the Bobby Clarke team MVP, stayed in the lineup after he suffered an unspecified arm injury in Game 3. Vladar took off both days of the series break and showed no sign of any physical discomfort. Vladar, incredulously, let a mental gaffe cost the Flyers a goal only 63 seconds into the second period when he misplayed the puck behind the net. Well out of place, an aggressive Rakell jostled the puck free and poked in an empty-netter for the 2-0 lead for Pittsburgh's first multigoal lead of the series.

That is the kind of goal that ages poorly in a series this tight. Vladar has been outstanding all postseason — he is the team MVP for a reason — and a single misplay should not erase that. It is, however, the kind of thing that can shift the gravity in a series.

The good news for the Flyers is the math has not really changed. They still lead three to one.

NHL playoff history is still against Crosby and the Penguins. Only four teams that trailed 3-0 in a seven-game series have come all the way back to win — the 1942 Toronto Maple Leafs, 1980 New York Islanders, 2010 Philadelphia Flyers and 2014 Los Angeles Kings.

Four teams in NHL history. One of them was, hilariously, the 2010 Flyers, who did it to the Bruins. The point is, statistically, the Flyers are still overwhelmingly likely to advance. They go to Pittsburgh on Monday with the rookie Porter Martone, the nineteen-year-old who has been the runaway story of this series, looking to close it out.

The Flyers under Rick Tocchet have built an identity.

Tocchet worked wonders with the Flyers in his first season on the bench and it was his fiery postgame speech after an overtime win in March that sparked an R-rated rallying cry. The Flyers winked at the unprintable battle cry and gave away Game 4 T-shirts to every fan that read: "Puck Everybody."

The shirts went out, the home crowd showed up, and the Flyers still lost. That is a moment that will galvanize a locker room or rattle one. We will know which by Monday night.
