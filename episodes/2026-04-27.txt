Let's start with the Phillies, because the story here is bigger than yesterday's box score, and yet yesterday's box score told you almost everything you need to know.

Sunday in Atlanta. Chris Sale on the mound. Six innings, one hit, nine strikeouts, his season high. Matt Olson hit a three-run homer in the first. Eli White added a two-run shot in the second. By the time the Phillies got their feet under them, Sale was already in the dugout sipping a Gatorade and the bullpen was finishing the job. Final score, six to two. Aaron Nola took the loss, his third of the season, and now sits at one and three. Kyle Schwarber hit his ninth home run of the year — the only real punch the Phillies landed — and that was that.

Here is the number that haunts this team. Nine and nineteen. They snapped a ten-game losing streak on Saturday, the longest losing streak this organization has had since nineteen ninety-nine, and the very next day they ran into Sale and dropped right back into the cellar. They are zero and ten this year against left-handed non-opener starters. Ryan, that is not a slump. That is a structural problem. Manager Rob Thomson is going to have to wear that, the front office is going to have to wear that, and the lineup, eventually, is going to have to wear that, because right now there is a glaring platoon hole that opposing managers have figured out and are exploiting every single time they hand the ball to a southpaw.

A few bright spots, because we are not going to spend an entire segment in the abyss. Bryce Harper is starting to look like Bryce Harper again. He had a four-RBI day in Saturday's eight-to-five extra-inning win — an infield single in the fifth, a sacrifice fly in the eighth, and a two-run single in the tenth — and on the year he is now slashing two-seventy, three-fifty-seven, five-twenty, with six homers and eighteen RBI. That is the kind of stat line that, if it sustains, drags this offense out of the basement. The problem is he is doing it largely alone. The team batting average is two-twenty, twenty-sixth in baseball. The on-base percentage is three-oh-one, also twenty-sixth. The slugging is three-sixty, twenty-fourth. There is no nice way to dress that up. This is one of the worst offensive starts a contending Phillies team has had in years.

The good news, and there is some, is that Zack Wheeler is back. He made his first big-league start since August fifteenth of last year on Saturday in Atlanta, and he punched out six in his return from the surgery to address venous thoracic outlet syndrome. He had been shaky in his five-game minor-league rehab — a five-eighty-five ERA, but with a twenty-three to five strikeout-to-walk ratio that told you the stuff was still there. Saturday, Ryan, the stuff was there. He didn't go deep, he wasn't perfect, but the fastball had its life back and the slider had its bite, and for a rotation that has been carrying water for the offense, getting Wheeler back is the closest thing to a reinforcement this team is going to see in April.

The other rotation note is Andrew Painter. The rookie right-hander is working through the kind of bumps you would expect from a young arm finding his major-league footing. He has surrendered at least three runs in three of his last four starts, and he carries a five-twenty-five ERA into his next outing. With Taijuan Walker out of the picture, Painter stays in the rotation. The Phillies are committed to letting him learn at this level, and frankly, given the alternative options at Lehigh Valley, they have to be. There is no quick fix coming from Triple-A.

On the injury front, Jonathan Bowlan, the right-handed reliever who has been on the fifteen-day IL since April thirteenth with a right groin strain, started his minor-league rehab assignment with Lehigh Valley on Saturday. He is eligible to come off the IL tomorrow, and there is a real chance he is back in the Phillies bullpen by midweek. Orion Kerkering, working his way back from a Grade One right hamstring strain that opened the season, is the next domino. Tristan McFarlane, the number nineteen prospect, was called up Friday, never got into a game, and was sent back to Double-A Reading on Saturday to clear roster space for Wheeler's activation. Standard April roster shuffle.

The schedule, mercifully, comes home tonight — well, tomorrow night. The Phillies are off today. They open a three-game set with the San Francisco Giants tomorrow at Citizens Bank Park. Jesús Luzardo, who is one and three with a six-ninety-one ERA — yikes — gets the ball against Tyler Mahle, who is one and three with a five-twenty-six. Two left-handers throwing batting practice to each other on the surface, but here is the lurking subplot. Luzardo has been the Phillies' biggest disappointment in the rotation. The trade for him was supposed to give this club a power left-hander to pair with Wheeler and Sánchez and make this the deepest staff in the National League. Instead, he is getting hit, he is walking guys, and tomorrow night feels like the start of a make-or-break stretch for him. If you cannot throw strikes against a Giants offense that is itself middling, the conversations about a bullpen move or a Triple-A reset are going to start.

The bigger Phillies story to watch this week, Ryan, is the temperature in the room. They came home from a road trip where they lost ten in a row. They snapped it Saturday. They lost again Sunday. They are nine and nineteen with the All-Star Game coming to Citizens Bank Park in July. Dave Dombrowski has not made any public moves yet, but the speculation, as the team's own house outlet phrased it on the website overnight, persists. That is the word — persists. Something gives soon. A lineup tweak, a bullpen call-up beyond Bowlan, a message-sending move. This roster is too talented and too expensive for nine and nineteen to be the ceiling of patience.

Let's move to the Eagles, because while the draft is over, the work is clearly not.

Sunday afternoon, Howie Roseman stood at his post-Day Three press conference and, in his careful, lawyer-with-a-clipboard way, told everyone listening that the safety position is still on the to-do list. The Eagles took Texas Tech's Cole Wisniewski in the seventh round, pick two-forty-four, after passing on the position in every earlier round. Wisniewski projects, realistically, as a depth option and a special teams contributor. He is not the answer. Roseman, to his credit, did not pretend otherwise. He acknowledged that Philadelphia still has to improve there, and used a phrase that I think is going to get a lot of mileage on Philly talk radio this week. He said the Eagles probably have a different vision of their safety room than the public does, but that there is still work to be done.

That is front-office speak for: we are going to trade for one. Jimmy Kempski wrote it plainly at PhillyVoice on Sunday — the Eagles are probably going to have to acquire a safety, because the realistic veteran options on the open market are thin to nonexistent. He floated the Browns' room as a logical trade partner, with Grant Delpit and Ronnie Hickman as the two names making the most sense given Cleveland's cap situation and Philadelphia's need. The current depth chart, Ryan, is a little uncomfortable when you look at it cold. Andrew Mukuba, last year's second-year pro, is the top safety on the roster — a five-eleven, one-eighty-six player who missed half his rookie season to injury. Behind him are Marcus Epps and Sydnei Carter, both of whom were essentially discarded by their previous teams. That is not a championship safety room. Not in this division, not facing the receivers Philadelphia faces twice a year.

The flip side of the draft was the trade for Jonathan Greenard, and that is being received around the league as the kind of move that gets a draft weekend remembered for the right reasons. The Eagles sent the Vikings their third-round pick this year, the ninety-eighth overall, plus a twenty twenty-seven third, and got Greenard plus pick two-forty-four. Then, crucially, they signed him to a contract extension on the spot. Greenard was second in the entire NFL in total pressures in twenty twenty-four. Pair him with Jalen Carter, Jaelan Phillips — who was acquired earlier in the offseason from Miami — and Nolan Smith, and the Eagles' pass-rush rotation is suddenly the deepest it has been since the Super Bowl run. Greenard told reporters on draft night that Jalen Hurts had already texted him about another championship. That is the energy in that building right now.

Let's run quickly through the rest of this draft class, because there are some interesting names. The first-round pick was wide receiver Makai Lemon out of USC, taken twentieth overall after Philadelphia traded up three spots with Dallas — yes, the Cowboys helped the Eagles move up — to get him. Lemon profiles as a slot-heavy, high-volume target who pairs with DeVonta Smith and signals, pretty plainly, that A.J. Brown is on his way out. The Brown trade, at this point, feels like a matter of when, not if. The second-round pick was Texas A&M tight end Eli Stowers, who is a pass-catcher first and a blocker fifth. Given how the tight end position bled the Eagles last year, that's a calculated bet. Third round, Texas A&M offensive tackle Markel Bell, who is six foot nine and a developmental project. Fourth and fifth rounds were quarterback depth and offensive line depth, and then in the seventh round Philadelphia took Uar Bernard, an athlete from Nigeria who has, and I am not making this up, never played football in his life. International Pathway program, total swing for the fences.

The undrafted free agent class, per Kempski's tracker, is headlined by Alabama linebacker Deontae Lawson — a three-year starter who wore the green dot for the Crimson Tide defense and who tore an ACL late in twenty twenty-four. If healthy, Lawson should not have gone undrafted. He is the most likely UDFA on this roster to make the fifty-three.

Stepping back, here is the analytical read. The Eagles entered this draft with a near-championship roster and a few specific holes. They filled the pass-rush hole with the Greenard trade. They filled the receiver-of-the-future hole with Lemon. They began to address tight end with Stowers. They added depth at offensive line, quarterback, and special teams. The two boxes they did not check, and that Roseman conceded, are starting safety and starting offensive tackle. Both, almost certainly, are going to require veteran moves between now and training camp. Watch the Browns. Watch a few veteran tackles who could become cap casualties in May and June. The Eagles are not done shopping.

Let's move to the Sixers, because Ryan, what we watched on Sunday night at Xfinity Mobile Arena was one of those games that crystallized exactly where this series, and maybe this team, is right now.

Boston one-twenty-eight, Philadelphia ninety-six. The Celtics now lead the series three games to one. Game Five is tomorrow night in Boston.

The Joel Embiid storyline was the entire pregame. He had his appendectomy on April ninth, in Houston, after being stricken with appendicitis overnight. He had not played a game since April sixth. He was upgraded from doubtful, then cleared roughly forty minutes before tipoff, walked out wearing a protective brace around his midsection, and started. And for about twelve minutes, it worked. He shot three-of-five in the first quarter while the rest of the Sixers missed ten of thirteen, and he finished the night with twenty-six points, ten rebounds, and six assists in thirty-four minutes. By any reasonable medical standard, that is a remarkable line seventeen days after abdominal surgery.

But basketball, especially playoff basketball, is not graded on remarkable medical lines. It is graded on wins. And the Sixers got dismantled. Payton Pritchard came off the Boston bench and dropped thirty-two points, including six made threes. Boston as a team made twenty-four three-pointers. Twenty-four. Jayson Tatum had thirty points and eleven assists, continuing what has become an emotional comeback story for him after his ruptured Achilles last May. Jaylen Brown added twenty points. The Celtics outrebounded Philadelphia fifty-one to thirty. That is the part that is hard to swallow if you are Nick Nurse. Boston physically punished this team on the glass while shooting the lights out.

Tyrese Maxey did what he could, but the rest of the supporting cast did not show up. Quentin Grimes, who has now totaled fifteen points across the entire series, has gone genuinely cold at the worst possible time — five points a game on twenty-something minutes a night. Justin Edgecombe got his second straight double-double with ten points and ten rebounds, but on five-of-seventeen shooting and zero-of-seven from three. Paul George logged a season-high forty-one minutes and basically played the entire second half, which tells you what you need to know about the Sixers' bench depth at this stage of the year.

Nick Nurse said after the game it is going to have to be a big mental pickup heading into Game Five. He is right, and he is also coaching against history. Teams up three to one in an NBA playoff series have advanced ninety-five point six percent of the time. Thirteen teams in NBA history have come back from three-one. Embiid is now seventeen days post-appendectomy, with the brace, in Boston, against a Celtics team that has thumped Philadelphia by thirty-two points in two of the four games. The math is unforgiving.

The honest read here, Ryan, is that this Sixers season has been defined by the same theme as every recent Sixers season: an Embiid health calendar that simply does not align with the playoff calendar. He had a knee issue earlier this year. He had the appendectomy. He returned in time to play, but not in time to ramp up. Around him, the supporting cast is a collection of fits and not-quite-fits — George, who was acquired for exactly this moment, putting up valiant minutes but clearly compromised; Maxey, brilliant but thirty-one-points-and-a-loss brilliant; a bench that has produced almost nothing all series; and a roster construction that, fairly or not, is going to be re-litigated all summer.

Game Five tomorrow night in Boston. Eight Eastern. The Sixers are going to need Embiid to be even better than he was Sunday, Maxey to be transcendent, and at least one of the role players — Grimes, Oubre Jr., Edgecombe — to remember he is a basketball player. It is a lot to ask. Ryan, do not bet the mortgage.

Let's move to the Flyers, because that series, somehow, is still alive, and tonight is the night they can put the Penguins out of their misery.

The setup is this. Philadelphia took Game One three to two. Took Game Two three to nothing on the road. Took Game Three five to two at home. Up three games to none, in the so-called Battle of Pennsylvania, the first playoff meeting between these two teams since twenty eighteen. Then on Saturday night, in front of their own crowd in South Philly, the Flyers ran into the wall every team that has ever tried to sweep Sidney Crosby has run into. Crosby opened the scoring. He set up Kris Letang's eventual game-winner. He picked up his two hundred and fourth career playoff point. The Penguins won four to two, forced a Game Five, and now everything moves to Pittsburgh.

Game Five is tonight, seven Eastern, on ESPN, at PPG Paints Arena. If the Flyers win, they advance to the second round and probably await the winner of the Hurricanes' next series — Carolina, by the way, became the first team in the entire NHL to advance, sweeping Ottawa four to nothing, with Logan Stankoven tying a franchise record by scoring in his fourth straight playoff game. That is the Flyers' likely next opponent if they finish this off tonight.

Here is what's interesting about how the Flyers got here. This was not supposed to be the year. Philadelphia entered the playoffs as the third seed in the Metropolitan Division, a younger team than most of the other contenders, and the matchup with Pittsburgh felt, on paper, like a coin flip leaning slightly toward experience. The Penguins still have Crosby, Malkin, Letang. They still have a coach who has been there. And yet, through four games, the Flyers have been the better five-on-five team in three of them. The goaltending has held up. The young forwards have played without fear. Tonight is the test of all of that. Game Five on the road, with a desperate Crosby playing the best hockey of his late career, and a Penguins crowd that knows this might be the end of the road for one of the great cores in franchise history.

What I will be watching tonight, beyond the obvious. First, the Flyers' power play. They have generated chances all series but they have not buried them in volume, and the difference between a tight third period and a comfortable third period is one extra power-play goal. Second, the special-teams discipline. Saturday's loss came in part because Philadelphia spent too much time short-handed and Pittsburgh's veterans capitalized. Third, the Crosby line. If the Flyers can break even when Crosby is on the ice and feast against the rest of the Penguins' forwards, they win this game. Crosby is going to do Crosby things — that is now a given — but the rest of the Pittsburgh roster is older and slower and has been chasing the puck for most of this series.

If they don't close it tonight, by the way, Game Six is Wednesday at the Wells Fargo Center, and Game Seven, if it gets that far, is Saturday in Pittsburgh. Better to do it tonight. Series like this, you remember the team that left the door open as much as the team that walked out alive.
