Ryan's Sports Podcast — Monday, May 18, 2026
============================================================

Good morning, Ryan. The Phillies finished the job in Pittsburgh, and they did it the way contending teams finish jobs — with their ace on the mound, their MVP at the plate, and not a single mistake in seven innings of work.

Zack Wheeler pitched seven sparkling innings, Bryce Harper hit a home run off ace Paul Skenes, and the Philadelphia Phillies beat the Pittsburgh Pirates six to nothing on Sunday for a three-game sweep that moved them over five hundred for the first time since early April.

One sweep, one statement, one team that finally looks like itself again.

Welcome in. It is Monday, May the eighteenth, the start of a new week, and for the first time in what feels like a long stretch, every flagship Philadelphia franchise has a reason to walk into Monday with its chin up. The Phillies are over five hundred and surging. The Eagles have their full 2026 schedule and a roster that just locked in another year of Saquon Barkley. The Flyers, eliminated in the second round, are now navigating their first real offseason with playoff expectations attached. The Sixers are gone from the postseason but have a front-office shake-up and a draft pick to plan around. And the NBA's marquee Western Conference Finals tip off tonight in Oklahoma City, with Shai Gilgeous-Alexander officially crowned a back-to-back MVP.

Here is what is on the docket. We are going to start with the Phillies, because that sweep in Pittsburgh is the most interesting thing happening in Philly sports right now, and the Don Mattingly story is starting to feel less like a curiosity and more like the season's defining inflection point. Then we move to the Eagles — the schedule release is fresh and the path through 2026 is loaded with storylines. From there, the Sixers and the lingering aftershocks of the Daryl Morey firing, the Flyers and Danny Brière's offseason agenda, and a brisk tour around the leagues including tonight's Game Seven in Buffalo and Game One in Oklahoma City. Let's get to it.

Start where the Phillies are.

Philadelphia improved to twenty-four and twenty-three on Sunday and has now won seven of their last eight, going fifteen and four since Don Mattingly replaced Rob Thomson as manager on April twenty-eighth.

Sit with that number for a second. Fifteen and four. In a sport where the very best teams play around six hundred ball over a full season, the Phillies under Mattingly have played close to eight hundred. It is a sample, not a verdict, but it is a sample large enough to matter.

The most important thing about Sunday, though, was who delivered it.

Wheeler picked up his third win without a loss, allowing four hits while striking out eight and walking one, and his ERA dropped to one point nine nine through six starts since beginning the season on the injured list while recovering from surgery to remove a blood clot from his upper right arm.

That is genuinely remarkable. A pitcher coming back from a vascular procedure has every reason to need a half-season to find form. Wheeler skipped the on-ramp. He came back and immediately looked like the best version of himself.

And he did it on Sunday against the toughest possible measuring stick. Paul Skenes is the standard for a young starting pitcher in this sport, and Wheeler outdueled him in his own park.

Bryce Harper took Skenes deep with a solo home run in the sixth inning, his twelfth of the year

, and from there the lineup piled on.

Bryson Stott followed two batters later with a bases-loaded double off Isaac Mattson, driving in two runs to blow the game open

, and the rout was on.

Stott finished the day with three RBI and his fourth home run of the season, while Harper drove in the thirtieth run of his season.

The texture of this win is what matters. The Phillies were not bludgeoning a bad team. They were beating a Pirates club that had played them tough, in a series where Skenes was on the mound for the rubber game, and they did it cleanly — Wheeler at his best, Harper square on the barrel, Stott extending the lineup, and the bullpen doing maintenance work on a six-run lead. This is what a healthy contender looks like.

Now the lens shifts forward. Tonight begins a three-game home series with the Cincinnati Reds at Citizens Bank Park, and the matchup is fascinating for one reason.

Andrew Painter, the rookie right-hander now sporting a one-and-four record with a six-point-two-one ERA, gets the ball Monday night against left-hander Nick Lodolo, who comes in zero-and-one with an eight-point-six-eight mark.

Two struggling young arms, both teams looking for one of them to find a rhythm.

For Painter, this is the next chapter of the most important development storyline in the organization. The Phillies' patience with him is rooted in long-term belief, but the short-term box score has not been pretty. The Mattingly approach to handling Painter has already drawn attention — there have been quick hooks, there has been explicit messaging about protecting the player from himself. We will see again tonight whether Mattingly leaves him in to work through traffic, or pulls the chute the moment things tilt. That decision, more than the linescore, is the thing to watch.

The bigger picture for the Phillies, though, is just how dramatically the temperature has changed. Three weeks ago this was a fan base seriously debating whether Rob Thomson had lost the room. Now it is a fan base watching Wheeler look like a Cy Young candidate, Harper banging at his career rate, Stott driving in runs at a clip he never reached in 2025, and a manager who has clearly given the clubhouse a new pulse. There is a long way to go. The National League East has not gotten easier — the Mets and Braves are not going anywhere, and the schedule will toughen up. But the version of the Phillies that limped through the first three weeks of April is gone, replaced by a club that looks rested, focused, and quite a bit deeper than people gave it credit for.

A note on health and the depth chart, because it is going to matter. The rotation now has Wheeler healthy and dominant, which solves the most important problem the team had at the start of the year. Aaron Nola has settled. Painter is the development project. The lineup has Harper at thirty RBI in mid-May, Trea Turner producing, Alec Bohm contributing, and Brandon Marsh holding his own. Down at Triple-A and Double-A, there is a steady drip of news about prospects who are knocking on the door — the Phillies' top pitching prospect has stacked nine-strikeout outings in back-to-back starts to open his Double-A line. That is not a tonight problem, but it is the kind of organizational depth that contenders lean on in July and August.

Bottom line on the Phillies. Sweep complete, ace humming, manager justifying every ounce of front-office faith placed in him, and the team headed home above five hundred with the most attainable home series of the next two weeks staring them in the face. Lock in tonight. Painter on the mound. First pitch around seven oh-five.

To the Eagles, where there is no game to recap but plenty of business to navigate. The biggest piece of news in the last few days is the 2026 schedule, which dropped Thursday night, and the more time you spend with it the more interesting it gets.

The Eagles, two-time defending NFC East champions, open the regular season at Lincoln Financial Field on Sunday, September the thirteenth, hosting the Washington Commanders at four-twenty-five on Fox. It is the second straight year Philadelphia opens at home, and the first Week One meeting between the two franchises since 2019, when the Eagles won thirty-two to twenty-seven.

A Howie Roseman team almost always benefits from opening at home, and getting Washington Week One — before Jayden Daniels and the new Commanders staff have fully gelled — is a soft landing into the season.

Then it gets harder, fast.

Week Two is at Tennessee at one o'clock on Fox. Week Three is at Chicago on Monday Night Football at eight-fifteen on ESPN. Week Four is the Rams at home at one o'clock. Week Five is the London trip to face the Jaguars at nine-thirty in the morning on NFL Network.

The London game shows up earlier on the calendar than usual, which is something.

Philadelphia will travel to Tottenham Hotspur Stadium to face Jacksonville on October the eleventh, at nine-thirty in the morning Eastern Time on NFL Network. It is only the third international regular-season game in franchise history, and the second against Jacksonville overseas.

From there, the run hits the prime-time gauntlet.

Week Six is Carolina at home. Week Seven is Dallas at home on a Monday night at eight-fifteen on ESPN. Week Eight is the Sunday night return to Washington at eight-twenty on NBC. Week Nine is the Giants at home. The Eagles bye comes in Week Ten.

Five primetime windows, two holiday games, and a finishing kick that will define the seeding race.

Week Eleven is Pittsburgh at home at four-twenty-five on CBS. Week Twelve is Thanksgiving at Dallas at four-thirty on Fox. Week Thirteen is at Arizona. Week Fourteen is Indianapolis at home. Week Fifteen is the Saturday game against Seattle at five o'clock on Fox. Week Sixteen is Christmas Eve against Houston at eight-fifteen on Amazon Prime. Week Seventeen is the Sunday night trip to San Francisco at eight-twenty on NBC. Week Eighteen finishes at the Giants on a date to be determined.

That December stretch is brutal. Four games in fourteen days against Indianapolis, Seattle, and Houston, all of whom were playoff teams a year ago, with the trip to San Francisco capping it.

Eight games against playoff teams from a year ago, with NFC East rivalry games on top of that.

The depth of this schedule is no joke.

The other layer to the Eagles' offseason is the scheme question.

Philadelphia reworked basically their entire offensive staff in the wake of last year's struggles, led by the hiring of new offensive coordinator Sean Mannion. The new approach is expected to involve a new run scheme, with added emphasis on play-action, pre-snap motion, and more under-center concepts.

If you remember the back half of 2025, that is exactly the prescription. The Eagles tried to win games where Jalen Hurts was asked to carry the offense from shotgun, and it did not produce. The Mannion philosophy is more diverse, more deliberate, and theoretically a better fit for Saquon Barkley.

About Barkley — the context-setting numbers are important.

Barkley managed eleven hundred and forty rushing yards on four-point-one yards a carry, with his seventy-one-point-three rushing yards per game dwarfed by the previous year's league-leading one-twenty-five-point-three. He had three one-hundred-yard rushing games, compared to eleven in 2024.

The 2025 version was still a very good NFL running back. It just was not the historic version Eagles fans saw the year before. Whether that was the scheme, the offensive line health, or some natural regression to the mean is the central question of the Eagles' 2026 offense.

It was obvious that the offensive line wasn't opening up holes for him like it did in 2024. Barkley led NFL running backs in yards-before-contact with three-point-eight in 2024, and that number was down to two-point-five in 2025.

That yards-before-contact gap is the most diagnostic number in the entire conversation. When Barkley's runs started 1.3 yards earlier in 2024 than in 2025, the entire downstream production followed. Mannion's job, scheme job number one, is to get those numbers back up.

One other note from the offseason chatter that is worth filing away. There has been continued whisper-level reporting about the league wondering whether the Eagles would consider moving on from A.J. Brown, and at minimum it is the kind of thing that gets floated around schedule release week. Roseman has historically been comfortable being uncomfortable, but trading away a top-five receiver inside a Super Bowl window is not the kind of decision this front office tends to make. File it under "discussed but unlikely."

The big takeaway on the Eagles. The schedule sets up beautifully through Week Nine — they should be favored in most of those games — and then there is a brutal stretch from Thanksgiving onward that will likely decide whether they're a one-seed or a three-seed. Combine that with a new offensive staff installing a fundamentally different scheme, a healthy Barkley, and an improved cornerback room with Riq Woolen added, and it is hard to argue this is anything other than a legitimate NFC contender again.

To the Sixers. The basketball part of the year is over, but the front-office part of the year is just getting interesting.

The Sixers fired Daryl Morey on Tuesday and have turned over player-personnel power, at least for now and perhaps for longer, to Bob Myers.

Six years of Morey, a couple of conference semifinal cameos, an awful lot of cap maneuvering, and now Myers — the architect of the Warriors dynasty during the Steph Curry years — is the man in the chair.

The simple read on this move is that Josh Harris has decided that the current core needs a fresh voice making the resource-allocation decisions. The cynical read, which Mike Sielski expressed in the Inquirer this weekend, is that nothing actually changes regardless of who is in the chair. He pointed out a history of regime turnover at the Sixers — Collins, DiLeo, Brown, Rivers, Hinkie, Brand — and argued that the franchise's structural issues have outlived every front-office reshuffle to date. That is a fair piece of skepticism to keep in mind.

What we know about the practical situation.

The Sixers have the twenty-second overall pick in the 2026 NBA Draft.

They have a first-round pick in this draft because of the Jared McCain trade with Oklahoma City, in which they acquired a first-round pick via the Houston Rockets and three second-rounders at the trade deadline back in February.

They already owed their own first-round pick to the Oklahoma City Thunder, which became the seventeenth overall pick after the lottery, and they are set to receive the twenty-second pick via the Houston Rockets thanks to the McCain trade.

The 2026 draft outlook from where they are sitting is not a needle-mover in isolation.

The franchise made the postseason just a year after winning only twenty-four games and pulled off an upset of the rival Boston Celtics, but the highs didn't last long. They flamed out in four games to the New York Knicks, and yet again, several key players missed long stretches of the season.

The Embiid availability question is the question that hovers above every other question. Until the front office knows what they have in their franchise center for the next two seasons, every other decision is essentially provisional.

The mock draft chatter around pick twenty-two has trended toward big men and connector wings.

Bleacher Report has the Sixers selecting Houston's Chris Cenac Jr., a player they describe as both a long-term Embiid replacement candidate and a short-term frontcourt complement.

ESPN's Jeremy Woo has them taking Santa Clara forward Allen Graves, a player with a strong analytic profile. Other names in the range include Cameron Carr from Baylor and Henri Veesaar from North Carolina. None of those names solve any major problem; all of them might be useful rotation pieces.

The Bob Myers introductory press conference, by the way, is going to set the tone for everything. The early read from people who heard it was that Myers — true to the Warriors playbook — emphasized roster construction around stars, role-player development, and a less transactional approach than the Morey era. We will see whether that is rhetoric or whether it actually changes how the Sixers approach free agency in July. Three of the rotation pieces from this year's team are unrestricted free agents this summer, and how Myers handles those re-signings will be the first real test.

Coach Nick Nurse, for what it is worth, survived the front-office change.

The Sixers quieted brief speculation that head coach Nick Nurse could be let go after last season's disastrous twenty-four-and-fifty-eight campaign. Now that Nurse has gone three seasons without a deep playoff run, it is easier to hear the whispers about his seat warming.

Beating the Celtics in seven games in round one bought him another year. The Knicks sweep should, in fairness, not be entirely on Nurse — that was a bad matchup with two important players hurt. But year four of Nurse is going to need to look very different from years one through three.

Two final Sixers notes. Joel Embiid declared at the end of the year that he is feeling confident about his knee. That is the entire ballgame. If Embiid plays seventy games next year, this team is a top-four seed in the East. If he plays forty, none of the rest of this matters. And the Paul George contract — the second-most-discussed contract on the books — is going to be looked at very carefully by Myers and his team this summer. Trade markets for thirty-five-plus players on max money are thin, but they exist.

To the Flyers, where the season ended over a week ago in Carolina but the conversation has only intensified.

Briere wrapped up the season, the team's first playoff appearance since the pandemic bubble of 2020, by thanking Flyers fans for their passion and patience. The Flyers were moved by the spontaneous standing ovation they received at the end of their Eastern Conference Semifinal series with the Carolina Hurricanes. Carolina won all four games in the series, two were in overtime.

That ovation, by the way, is the through line.

Briere said in his exit press conference that seven or eight guys in his exit meetings specifically mentioned how the fans reacted at the end of the game. The comment that kept coming back was that they knew how special it was to play in the playoffs in Philadelphia, but they didn't realize how special it was. The fans really made it that extra special.

That is the emotional residue of this season. The hockey part is a different story.

The Flyers earned respect for their resiliency and heart in a ninety-eight-point regular season, followed by a first-round victory over the archrival Pittsburgh Penguins.

First playoff series win in a generation, basically. Then they ran into Carolina, the team that is now eight and zero through these playoffs and the consensus best team in the league.

The offseason agenda from Brière is now clarified.

The center position is something the Flyers are probably going to look at. If they can improve on defense, they are going to look at that too. So, not much has changed from years past, other than maybe the goaltending position.

Dan Vladar's emergence in goal was the rare offseason move from a year ago that worked exactly as advertised, and the contract extension talk around him is already swirling.

Brière said he has no worries about re-signing restricted free agents Trevor Zegras and defenseman Jamie Drysdale, and there have been rumors already that the Flyers are interested in extending Vladar.

The Michkov situation is the loudest noise around the franchise.

The Matvei Michkov situation has been talked about ad nauseam, but the fact is the Flyers need to bridge the disconnect with the Russian winger and find a way to make this work. While Michkov was demonstratively frustrated during his exit interview, things don't appear to be at a code red level yet regarding his long-term future with the organization.

Michkov has not decided where he will train this summer, whether in Voorhees or Russia, but did say he will spend some time in New Jersey. He wants to make sure he's ready to go, and the team made clear that training camp next year is shorter, not a lot of time to ease into it.

That last bit is the most important sentence. Michkov's roller-coaster sophomore season ended with him being a healthy scratch at one point and underwhelming in the playoffs, and the message from the team is unambiguous — show up in shape, day one, or there is no runway. He remains the Flyers' most talented forward and their most important long-term piece. He just has not yet been a consistent everyday player.

The injury report Brière dropped Thursday is also worth marking.

The list included fractured wrists, MCL sprains, fractured ribs, internal bleeding, a separated shoulder, fractured fibulas, and a list of names from Andrae and Bump to Cates, Dvorak, Hathaway, Konecny, Tippett, York, and Zegras.

Ten players, real injuries, all played through it. That is the kind of context a casual fan does not always see during a playoff run.

While Owen Tippett may need a procedure to resolve a sports hernia, he should be ready to start training camp on time, and his more concerning issue of internal bleeding has been resolved. Other Flyers players who need offseason surgeries, such as Emil Andrae, should be ready for training camp come September.

The only player Brière flagged as a real question to start next season is Nikita Grebenkin, whose upper-body injury could linger into camp.

Mike Sielski put forward an interesting name to track.

Seattle Kraken center Shane Wright fits the profile of the type of player Brière likes to acquire.

The Brière formula has been a clear one — buy low on talented young players whose original teams have soured on them.

From Trevor Zegras to David Jiricek, from Owen Tippett-for-Claude Giroux to Jamie Drysdale-for-Cutter Gauthier, Brière has either supported or consummated trades for talented young players who were underperforming with other teams.

The Inquirer's offseason scouting list also threw out larger names —

a move for Auston Matthews seems unrealistic given his ability to dictate his next destination, but established top-of-the-lineup centers and former All-Stars like Robert Thomas, Nico Hischier, and Matty Beniers could all be available for the right price.

Don't expect a splash for splash's sake. Brière said as much: the rebuild ethos has not changed, even if the expectations have.

Around the leagues now. Quick hits.

In the NBA, tonight is the big one.

The conference finals begin on Monday, May the eighteenth, with the Thunder and Spurs in Game One.

San Antonio enters at sixty-two and twenty behind Victor Wembanyama's dominant two-way play, and the series marks the first matchup since the 1998 NBA Finals featuring two teams with at least sixty-two regular-season wins.

The basketball stakes are enormous.

The Spurs dared Oklahoma City's trio of perimeter stoppers — Luguentz Dort, Cason Wallace, and Alex Caruso — to beat them with their jump shots during the teams' five regular-season meetings. It was a sensible strategy, prioritizing packing the paint to clog the driving lanes for Gilgeous-Alexander, often by having Wembanyama assigned to one of the role players and sagging far off of them.

It also worked. Caruso was five-for-twenty-one from three-point range against San Antonio, and Wallace was also five-for-twenty-one from long range with a team-worst minus-thirty-two in his minutes.

If those role players cannot make San Antonio pay this time, the Spurs will steal this series.

The other layer to the MVP coronation.

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander has won his second consecutive NBA Most Valuable Player award, becoming the fourteenth player in league history to win back-to-back MVPs.

According to Shams Charania, he is the NBA's first back-to-back MVP since Nikola Jokic in 2021 and 2022.

That is rarefied air. Eastern Conference Finals between the Knicks and Cavaliers tip Wednesday in New York.

In the NHL, tonight is Game Seven of Sabres at Canadiens.

Buffalo blew out Montreal eight to three in the Bell Centre on Saturday night to force a Game Seven. The winner moves on to face the Hurricanes in the Eastern Conference Final.

Game Seven will take place on Monday at seven-thirty Eastern at KeyBank Center.

Carolina, sitting eight and zero, will be a heavy favorite against either survivor. The Western Conference Final tips Wednesday in Colorado with the Avalanche hosting Vegas. The Avs swept the Kings, the Golden Knights ground out a six-game win over Anaheim, and the bookmakers have Colorado as the team to beat.

In MLB beyond the Phillies, the National League East shape is starting to come into focus. The Braves and Mets are still the consensus class of the division, but the Phillies' fifteen-and-four under Mattingly has them right in the middle of the conversation. Around the broader league, watch the AL East where Tampa and Toronto keep trading punches. And note that Mets pitcher Clay Holmes has a fractured fibula that he is hoping does not require surgery. The trade deadline market for relievers will be active.

In the NFL, Saquon Barkley is the loudest Eagles name but not the most newsworthy across the league this morning. Vegas appears to be planning around Fernando Mendoza after taking him first overall in the 2026 draft. Anthony Richardson reported to Colts voluntary workouts despite his earlier trade request. And the league announced Christmas tripleheader plans — Packers at Bears, Bills at Broncos, Rams at Seahawks. That Rams-Seahawks game has serious NFC seeding implications for the Eagles, as well.

One last quick note from the lottery.

The Clippers landed the number five overall pick via the Pacers, and their future seems far less bleak than it did prior to Sunday.

The 2026 draft has now reshuffled the long-term outlook for half a dozen franchises, and the Sixers' protected future pick conversations get a little more complicated as a result.

Time to bring it home. One reflection for you, Ryan. There is a version of being a Philadelphia sports fan where the four teams all carry weight at once, where every Monday you wake up to a real story on at least two fronts, and this is one of those Mondays. The Phillies look fixed. The Eagles look loaded. The Sixers are in front-office triage but they have a coach, a star, and a plan. The Flyers earned a standing ovation for a season that ended in the second round, which is not a sentence I would have written six months ago. Enjoy this stretch. The bad weeks always come back. Today is not one of them.
