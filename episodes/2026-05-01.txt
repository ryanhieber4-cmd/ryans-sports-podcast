Let's start with the Phillies, because what they did yesterday actually slid into the record books.

The Phillies swept the Giants in Thursday's doubleheader at Citizens Bank Park with a 3-2 walk-off victory in Game 1 and a 6-5 walk-off victory in 10 innings in Game 2, leading them to a 3-0 start under interim manager Don Mattingly.

Three games into the Mattingly era, three wins, and yesterday's came in a way that has not happened to this franchise in living memory.

Game one was the matinee.

Bryson Stott lined a tying triple with one out in the ninth inning and scored the winning run on a two-out infield single by rookie Justin Crawford, rallying the Philadelphia Phillies to a 3-2 victory over the San Francisco Giants in the first game of a doubleheader Thursday.

Kyle Schwarber launched his 350th career homer for the Phillies, who have won consecutive games for the first time since a four-game winning streak from March 31 to April 4.

Think about that. Yesterday was the first time all month the Phillies had stacked back-to-back wins outside of one early-season hot streak. They are eleven and nineteen. They were buried, the manager was just dismissed, and somehow they're suddenly playing the most clutch baseball in the National League.

The story of game one was also Cristopher Sánchez.

Phillies left-hander Cristopher Sánchez gave up doubles to the Giants' first two hitters, Heliot Ramos and Matt Chapman. Luis Arraez's grounder knocked in Ramos from third, and Chapman scored on Casey Schmitt's single to make it 2-0.

Sánchez settled in after that — and the Phillies bullpen, of all units, kept it close enough for the offense to scratch back in the ninth.

Adolis García reached on an infield single against Ryan Walker to start the bottom of the ninth. Brandon Marsh struck out before Stott tripled into the right-field corner on the ninth pitch of the at-bat, handing Walker his second blown save this season. Stott held at third base on Edmundo Sosa's groundout, then scored as Crawford barely beat out a slow grounder to shortstop Willy Adames for his third hit.

Then they did it again.

Trea Turner and Schwarber started Game 2 with back-to-back homers in the first inning. It was the seventh time in franchise history the Phillies hit back-to-back homers in the opening frame. It was Schwarber's second homer of the doubleheader, his third in four games and his 11th of the season.

The Giants chipped away, took the lead late, and the Phillies once again stared down a ninth-inning deficit.

Kyle Schwarber capped a 4-for-4 game with a two-out, game-tying hit in the ninth inning.

The historical fingerprints on this day are wild.

This was the first time since at least 1910 the Phillies have had a game-tying ninth-inning hit in both ends of a doubleheader.

Nineteen ten. Taft was president.

The Phillies walked off the Giants twice in one day for the first time since 1998, finishing a doubleheader sweep behind more late-game magic.

Schwarber's individual line was historic in its own right.

According to OptaSTATS, Schwarber became the first player since Lou Gehrig in 1935 to compile five or more hits, three or more walks, two or more doubles, two or more homers and zero strikeouts in a doubleheader.

First player since Gehrig. Read that twice. He's running so hot right now that it almost feels like the kind of run a free-agent-to-be willing himself into a contract. Whether or not that's the subtext, the on-field reality is that Schwarber is carrying this offense.

The other name to take with you out of the doubleheader is Chase Shugart.

He is the first Phillies pitcher to do so since Terry Adams on Sept. 21, 2002, vs. the Reds. This was the first time since July 31, 1983, that a team won on a walk-off in both games of a doubleheader with the same pitcher earning the win in both.

Shugart, a name most Phillies fans probably weren't sure how to pronounce two weeks ago, joined a list with Jesse Orosco. That's how strange this season has been so far.

A few connective threads worth pulling. One: the manager change.

It has opened similarly. On Tuesday, interim manager Don Mattingly took over for Thomson. The Phillies responded with a 7-0 shutout over San Francisco. Two days later, in the first game of Thursday's doubleheader, rookie Justin Crawford walked off the Giants in comeback fashion.

Three games, three wins, but the Phillies remain eleven and nineteen, which means they're eight games under and they were essentially playing for someone's job every night even before Thomson got moved off the bench. Mattingly hasn't reinvented the lineup. He's just been there for the moment the team's three biggest bats — Schwarber, Turner, and Stott — finally clicked back on at the same time.

Two: Zack Wheeler.

Philadelphia was set to open with LHP Tim Mayza in a bullpen game after RHP Zack Wheeler was scratched from his second scheduled start since returning from thoracic outlet decompression surgery last September.

That's the lingering medical concern hanging over this rotation. Wheeler scratched from a second straight start — that's a beat-watch story for the next several days. The Phillies cannot afford to lose their ace for an extended period given how the rotation has performed in April.

Three: Sánchez and contact. The Athletic-style read on Sánchez right now is that the surface ERA is fine, but the underlying contact rates aren't.

Entering the start, Cristopher Sánchez carried a 2.94 ERA despite allowing 44 hits in 33 2/3 innings. He was tied for second in the league in hits allowed per game. More than anything, that has spoken to his poise, especially as he has pitched to contact more often in 2026. In his previous start, the Cubs scattered 12 hits against him. Three starts earlier, the Giants got to him for 11.

He's the staff's most durable, dependable arm right now, but the ERA might not stick if the contact profile doesn't change.

Today the Phillies open up a series and try to push this winning streak to four. Long-term, this team needs to start banking series wins, not just dramatic individual games. The Mattingly honeymoon is fun. The hole is still there.

Let's switch gears to the Sixers, because what happened on Broad Street last night was as improbable as anything I can remember in the recent history of this franchise. Down three games to one against the second-seeded Celtics. Joel Embiid having had emergency appendicitis surgery in early April. Two losses by thirty-plus along the way. And yet — Game Seven, on the road in Boston, on Saturday night.

Up against the heavily favored Celtics, the Sixers outplayed, outcoached and outhustled Boston on their way to a 106-93 victory that wasn't that close. The Sixers have tied their first-round series up at three games apiece to force a Game 7 in Boston on Saturday.

The score flatters Boston. The Sixers led by nineteen entering the fourth quarter and Joe Mazzulla effectively raised the white flag.

Joe Mazzulla pulled his starters with over 10 minutes left and that Boston bench group actually went on an 11-0 run to briefly have "oh no" running through our minds. But the Sixers quickly course-corrected.

The headline is what Tyrese Maxey, Paul George, and Joel Embiid did together. Maxey, thirty points, five assists, two steals, zero turnovers. George, twenty-three and four with two steals and a block. And Embiid — and this is the part that's almost hard to process — Embiid just keeps playing.

Less than one week ago, we wouldn't have been surprised if Joel Embiid next took to a basketball court again in the fall. Instead, the big man has played the last three games following an emergency appendectomy, and could be in the midst of writing the most impressive playoff chapter of his career.

His shot wasn't there last night, but the impact was.

Although his shot wasn't falling on Thursday (just 6-of-18 from the field), Embiid positively impacted the game in so many ways. He utilized the excessive defensive attention on him to find open teammates time after time. The behind-the-back pass to a cutting Kelly Oubre on the baseline was masterful. Embiid would have had a triple-double with ease if the Sixers had been hitting shots at a normal clip during the first part of the game.

The play of the game — and probably of the series — came in the third quarter.

The play of the game - and maybe the series - came in the third when Kelly Oubre Jr. blocked Jaylen Brown, Maxey scooped the loose ball and fed to George on the break who then dazzled with a behind-the-back pass to VJ Edgecombe who finished with a thunderous dunk for a 69-54 lead.

A four-pass, four-player sequence that ended with a rookie throwing one down. That's playoff basketball at its purest.

George said the right things afterward, and they didn't sound like clichés.

"Just applaud the guys for not quitting," George said. "In this moment, we just stayed together. We stayed confident. We stayed poised. We've given ourselves a chance here, being down 3-1, with an unbelievable opportunity to go play a Game 7 in Boston."

Now let's talk about what this actually is, historically.

The only team in NBA history to win a playoff series after losing two games by 30-plus points was the Minneapolis Lakers in 1959 against the St. Louis Hawks.

That's the company the Sixers are chasing. The Celtics blew them out by thirty-plus twice in this series. Coming back from that, going to Boston and stealing Game Seven, would be one of the most improbable series wins in modern playoff history.

A word about Paul George, because he has been the defining underrated story of this run.

Lost in the shuffle at times is the 35-year-old George - in large part this season because of a 25-game suspension for flunking a drug test - who has deferred to the other three Sixers when needed yet can still flash that All-Star form. George hit a team-high five 3s that all stretched the lead and the offense ran through him when Embiid - still recovering from his early April surgery - was on the bench. George signed with the Sixers in the summer of 2024 on a four-year, $212 million free-agent contract and was expected to form a 1-2 championship punch with Embiid.

A year and a half into that contract, the punch hadn't really landed. Last night, in a desperate spot, in a city that had been ready to write him off, George looked like the version of himself you sign for two hundred and twelve million dollars.

Game Seven is Saturday night in Boston, seven thirty Eastern, on NBC. The Celtics are still the betting favorite to advance. Public and analytical models still think Boston wins this series the majority of the time. But the Sixers don't really care about that. They've already won three games most people thought they couldn't win. One more.

Cam York scored with 2:28 left in overtime, and the Philadelphia Flyers eliminated the Pittsburgh Penguins with a 1-0 win in Game 6 of the Eastern Conference First Round at Xfinity Mobile Arena on Wednesday.

Dan Vladar made 42 saves for the Flyers, who advanced in the Stanley Cup Playoffs for the first time since defeating the Montreal Canadiens in six games in the 2020 Eastern Conference First Round. It was their first series win in front of fans since eliminating the Penguins at home in Game 6 of the 2012 Eastern Conference Quarterfinals.

The image you'll keep is Cam York.

Cam York scored the Game 6 winner. It came with 2:30 minutes left in overtime. The Flyers' defenseman tossed his stick into the crowd before being mobbed by his teammates. "I hope everyone's OK in the stands, definitely don't want a lawsuit," York said with a laugh. "I honestly blacked out, I didn't even know what to do, I was so excited. Just so happy for my teammates and obviously the fans."

Vladar might be the bigger story, though.

Vladar was excellent in the series, allowing just 10 goals and delivering two shutouts.

York has been on him as the team MVP, and the work in this series — forty-two saves in a one-nothing winner-take-all overtime game — backs that up. The Flyers wouldn't be in round two without him.

Now they get a problem. The reward for surviving Pittsburgh is the league's best regular-season Eastern Conference team.

The Flyers, who are the No. 3 seed from the Metropolitan Division, will face the Carolina Hurricanes, the top seed in the East, in the second round.

Carolina swept Ottawa. They're rested. They're deep. And they had the Flyers' number for most of the regular season.

The Flyers and Hurricanes played each other four times in the 2025-26 regular season. Philadelphia did not lose to Carolina in regulation, going 1-0-3 with two shootout losses. The Flyers and Canes last met in Game 81, a 3-2 shootout win that clinched their first postseason berth in six years and secured the East's top seed for Carolina.

So in one sense the Flyers can hang their hat on never losing in regulation to this opponent. In another sense, you can read that record as Philadelphia hovering just below Carolina's level for an entire season.

The full schedule and exact time of the Flyers-Canes second-round series have yet to be announced. Flyers president of hockey operations Keith Jones told 94.1 WIP on Thursday morning that Saturday would be the "earliest" for Game 1.

So tomorrow, Sunday, or Monday in Raleigh for game one — and if you're picking the Sixers' Game Seven over the Flyers' Game One on Saturday night, well, welcome to Philadelphia in May.

A couple of pieces of analytical context for the matchup.

The Flyers quite literally did produce enough offensively to get through their first-round series, but 16 goals in six games against a Pittsburgh team that can be rather porous defensively was a bit underwhelming. In terms of the opposing defense, the Flyers are going up several weight classes this time.

The underlying numbers are even uglier.

Philadelphia mustered just 2.17 xGF/60 at 5-on-5, good for 14th out of the 16 teams in the first round. They also went 2-for-17 on the power play for an 11.8% conversion rate. After ranking dead last in the league in the regular season with a 15.7% success rate with the man advantage, it remains a glaring red flag.

The man-advantage problem is the one to watch. If you can't score on the power play, and you're playing a team like Carolina that pressures every special-teams sequence, the margin shrinks fast.

The other variable: Matvei Michkov.

After being healthy scratched in Game 5, Matvei Michkov had a couple of good looks during the second period. In a span of 29 seconds, he was denied on a semi breakaway and then at the post when he tried to tuck one past Silovs. Michkov and Cates assisted York's winner.

The Flyers need him to be a real factor against Carolina, because the Hurricanes can lock down the middle of the ice in ways the Penguins simply did not. There's only so much greasy, low-event hockey this team can win.

Eagles. Today is rookie minicamp day at the Jefferson Health Training Complex.

The Eagles are set to kick off the 2026 rookie minicamp on Friday, May 1, and Saturday, May 2, at the Jefferson Health Training Complex in Philly.

Eight draftees, plus undrafted free agents. The headline name: Makai Lemon, the wide receiver out of USC.

The contract news dropped yesterday.

New Philadelphia Eagles rookie Makai Lemon has become the first member of the 2026 NFL Draft class to sign his rookie deal.

On Thursday, April 30, ahead of rookie minicamp, Philadelphia and Lemon agreed to his rookie deal. Lemon becomes the first first-rounder to sign his first NFL contract this year. According to NFL insider Ian Rapoport, Lemon signed a four-year, fully guaranteed deal that includes an $11.5 million signing bonus.

Fully guaranteed, eleven and a half million in bonus, beat every other first-rounder to a signature. That's an alignment between team and player you don't always see this early in the process.

The price to acquire him was significant.

The Philadelphia Eagles traded up with the division rival Dallas Cowboys in last weekend's 2026 NFL Draft to select USC star wide receiver Makai Lemon with the No. 20 overall pick. The Eagles gave up two first-round picks to move from No. 23 to No. 20 overall, proving their excitement to land the offensive weapon.

Trading two first-rounders to move up three spots, and trading them to Dallas, is the sort of move that Howie Roseman tends to either own his draft board on or get roasted for. The early reads on what Lemon brings are encouraging.

Round 1, Pick 20: Makai Lemon, WR, USC Comparison: Amon-Ra St. He relies on elite route running ability and toughness to take hits over the middle, something the Eagles' first-rounder put on full display in college. Lemon has better timed speed (4.50 compared to St. Brown's 4.65) which the Eagles hope will allow him to play outside the numbers as well.

An Amon-Ra St. Brown comparison is on the optimistic end of the spectrum, but Brown's profile — slot-first, contested catches, willingness to work the middle — is closer to Lemon's college tape than the field-stretching outside type the Eagles already have on the roster.

His college production speaks for itself.

During his final season at USC, Lemon recorded 79 catches for 1,156 yards and 11 touchdowns.

Seventy-nine, eleven hundred fifty-six, eleven touchdowns. Now he plugs into a Jalen Hurts offense that has been searching for a third reliable receiving target alongside DeVonta Smith and A.J. Brown for several seasons.

The bigger Eagles draft picture is interesting too, because the most impactful move of the weekend wasn't a draft pick at all.

The Eagles acquired outside linebacker Jonathan Greenard and a seventh-round pick in 2026 (244th overall) from the Minnesota Vikings in exchange for a third-round draft pick this year (98th overall) and a 2027 third-round pick.

Greenard is a Pro Bowl-caliber edge rusher. The Eagles also added tight end Eli Stowers in the second round and shipped Sydney Brown to Atlanta in a small swap. The whole draft week is being read in two directions — Roseman built a richer skill-position room and a deeper edge rotation, but he paid for it with future capital.

No matter what the Eagles did on Day 3, the damage was done in the first two rounds.

That is one perspective. The other, more optimistic one, is that the Eagles are running it back as a true contender and weren't going to use that draft capital on roster builders anyway.

There's one structural piece I want to flag that's been a little under the radar.

On January 13, 2026, the Eagles parted ways with offensive coordinator Kevin Patullo after only one year in that role. On January 29, Sean Mannion was named the team's new offensive coordinator, marking the team's fifth consecutive year with a new offensive coordinator.

Five different offensive coordinators in five years is the sort of churn that can quietly become a real problem, especially for a quarterback who is now in his sixth NFL season and trying to maintain consistency. It's been masked by Hurts' own brilliance and Saquon Barkley's emergence, but it's something to monitor as Mannion settles into the role and starts installing his concepts at minicamp this weekend.

This will be the Eagles' first season since 2012 without offensive line coach & run game coordinator Jeff Stoutland, who announced his departure from the organization on February 4. Ryan Mahaffey was named the team's new run game coordinator & tight ends coach on February 6, and Chris Kuper was named the team's new offensive line coach.

Stoutland leaving is the bigger long-term loss than the coordinator merry-go-round, in my opinion. He was the keeper of an offensive line identity that produced multiple All-Pros. Whether Chris Kuper can preserve that culture is one of the most important questions of the next two training camps.

So today, watch for the first shots of Lemon in midnight green at minicamp. Watch for who's getting reps with the quarterbacks. And watch for the inevitable injury report — it's minicamp, but rookie hamstrings are usually the first beat-writer story to break.
